# StrategyScale Test Card

## Use case

UC04A - Fundraising - Raise Equity vs Debt vs Slow Growth

## Decision mode

Fundraising / Capital

## CEO decision

Should the CEO raise external equity, use debt, or slow the growth plan to preserve control?

## Buyer pain

The CEO wants to accelerate growth but is worried about dilution, control, valuation, debt burden and whether the business is genuinely ready to absorb capital. The board is pushing for ambition, but the founder/CEO is unsure whether raising money now creates freedom or simply adds pressure before the operating model is ready.

## Opening prompt

I am the CEO and majority shareholder of a 60-person B2B services and software business with £6.2m revenue, £1.1m EBITDA, and strong customer retention.

We provide a mix of recurring software, implementation support and advisory services to mid-market companies. The business has grown steadily through referrals and founder-led sales, but we now believe there is a larger opportunity if we invest properly in sales, product development and customer success.

The board is debating whether we should raise £2m of external equity, take on £1.25m of debt, or slow the growth plan and fund expansion from cash flow.

The opportunity is attractive:
- Revenue has grown from £3.8m to £6.2m over three years.
- EBITDA margin is around 18%.
- Net revenue retention is 112%.
- Customer churn is low.
- We have a strong product roadmap.
- Several larger customers have asked for features we cannot currently deliver quickly enough.
- A new sales leader believes we could double new-logo acquisition if we invest in a proper outbound team.

But there are concerns:
- The business is still dependent on me as CEO for major sales, partnerships and investor conversations.
- Our finance function is basic and reporting is not investor-grade.
- Product delivery is already stretched.
- We have not yet proven that outbound sales can work consistently.
- External equity would likely dilute me by 15–25%.
- Debt would preserve ownership but add pressure if growth takes longer than expected.
- Slower self-funded growth protects control but may allow competitors to move faster.

One investor has informally suggested a £10m pre-money valuation for a £2m raise. Our bank has indicated it may support a £1.25m term loan, subject to covenants and recurring revenue evidence.

Should we raise equity, use debt, or slow down and self-fund?

## Must-detect constraint

StrategyScale must identify that the core constraint is capital readiness, not capital availability.

The business may have funding options, but the real issue is whether the operating model, reporting, sales engine and delivery capacity are mature enough to convert capital into enterprise value rather than burn it into complexity.

## Good answer should broadly say

A strong answer should not simply say “raise equity because growth is attractive” or “use debt because dilution is bad”.

It should probably say something like:

Do not raise external equity yet. Use a staged funding approach: strengthen reporting, prove outbound sales, and use a smaller debt or internally funded growth sprint first. Raise equity only when the business can show that additional capital reliably converts into scalable ARR, not founder-led effort.

Depending on the answers, a good final recommendation may be:
- use a limited debt facility or self-funded 90–180 day proof sprint;
- delay equity until valuation and evidence improve;
- define investor-readiness milestones;
- avoid taking £2m equity at a valuation that prices today’s weaknesses rather than tomorrow’s potential;
- avoid over-leveraging before outbound sales and delivery capacity are proven.

## Weak answer failure mode

A weak answer would:
- treat this as a simple cost-of-capital comparison;
- recommend equity, debt or self-funding without testing operational readiness;
- over-focus on dilution percentages;
- ignore whether the business can absorb growth capital;
- assume outbound sales will work without proof;
- fail to identify founder dependency as a valuation drag;
- fail to create a staged decision gate.

Generic bad answer:

“Consider the pros and cons of equity versus debt and choose the option that best aligns with your risk appetite.”

That is not good enough.

## Sample answer set 1

Use these when StrategyScale asks the first batch of pressure-test questions.

### Answer 1 - Use of funds

Of the £2m equity raise, around £750k would go into building a sales team, £500k into product development, £300k into customer success and onboarding, £250k into finance, data and reporting, and the rest into working capital and contingency. The debt option would mainly fund sales hiring and some product development, but we would have less room for customer success and operational upgrades.

### Answer 2 - Sales proof

Outbound sales is not yet proven. We currently win most customers through referrals, partnerships and founder-led conversations. The new sales leader has built outbound before, but not in this exact market. We have run two small outbound tests: one created good meetings but poor conversion, and the other produced a few qualified opportunities but no signed customers yet.

### Answer 3 - Founder dependency

I am still involved in around 60% of larger new business conversations and nearly all strategic partnership discussions. Customers trust the broader team once onboarded, but I am still often used to close complex deals. If I stepped back tomorrow, new sales momentum would probably slow materially for at least six months.

### Answer 4 - Financial readiness

Our management information is improving but not yet investor-grade. We have good revenue and cash reporting, but margin by product line, cohort profitability, sales funnel metrics and implementation capacity reporting are still inconsistent. We could probably get investor-ready reporting in place within 3–4 months with the right finance support.

### Answer 5 - Debt capacity

The bank has indicated it could support a £1.25m term loan over four years. Indicative interest cost would be around 9–10%. Covenants would likely include minimum EBITDA, cash cover and recurring revenue retention. We could service the debt if growth continues steadily, but a failed sales build would make the covenants uncomfortable within 12–18 months.

## Sample answer set 2

Use these when StrategyScale asks the sharper second batch.

### Answer 1 - Valuation and dilution

The informal £10m pre-money valuation feels acceptable but not exciting. A £2m raise would dilute me by around 16.7% before any option pool expansion. If we could prove outbound sales and strengthen reporting over the next 6–9 months, I believe we could justify a higher valuation, perhaps £13m–£15m pre-money, but that is not guaranteed.

### Answer 2 - Product and delivery capacity

Product delivery is already stretched. We could absorb a controlled increase in demand, but if new-logo acquisition doubled quickly, onboarding and implementation would become the bottleneck. Customer success is also under-resourced, so growth without investment in onboarding could damage the strong retention profile that makes the business attractive.

### Answer 3 - Competitive urgency

There are two competitors moving faster. One has raised money and is hiring salespeople aggressively. The other has a weaker product but stronger marketing. We are not at immediate risk of losing the market, but if we wait 18–24 months without investing, we may lose some category visibility and partner mindshare.

### Answer 4 - Self-funded path

If we self-fund, we could invest around £450k–£600k over the next 12 months without putting the business under too much strain. That would allow us to hire two salespeople, add one product manager and improve finance reporting, but it would not support the full growth plan or a major product acceleration.

### Answer 5 - Personal control and risk appetite

I am willing to accept some dilution if it materially increases enterprise value, but I do not want to sell a meaningful stake before we have proved the growth engine. I would be frustrated if I gave up 15–25% and then discovered the bottleneck was not capital but sales execution, delivery capacity or my own founder dependency.

## Ideal final recommendation shape

A strong final output should land somewhere near:

CORE CALL:
Do not raise the full £2m equity round yet.

ONLY PROCEED IF:
Use the next 90–180 days to prove outbound sales, reduce founder dependency, and make reporting investor-grade.

COUNTER WITH:
A staged capital plan: self-fund or use a smaller debt facility for a proof sprint, then raise equity at a higher valuation once the business can prove capital converts into scalable growth.

## Proof test expectation

The proof test should create a clear decision gate, for example:

Within 180 days, prove that outbound sales can generate qualified pipeline, that the business can onboard new customers without damaging retention, and that reporting is investor-grade enough to support a stronger valuation.

Pass signals should include:
- outbound sales produces qualified pipeline and at least early signed revenue;
- founder involvement in new sales reduces materially;
- product/onboarding capacity has a realistic scale plan;
- reporting can show margin, funnel, retention and implementation capacity clearly;
- valuation logic improves.

Fail signals should include:
- outbound meetings do not convert;
- founder remains essential to closing deals;
- product or onboarding capacity becomes the bottleneck;
- debt covenants would become uncomfortable;
- investors price the round on founder dependency and immature reporting.
